The Experiment

By varying the channel width, we controlled difficulty: narrow channels = harder, wide channels = easier. But here's the key innovation: instead of following a fixed training sequence, an AI algorithm decided which difficulty to present next, based on each learner's real-time performance.

The Machine Learning Strategy

We used a multi-armed bandit algorithm â€” a technique that balances trying new challenges (exploration) with reinforcing what works (exploitation). The system continuously analyzed two key metrics:

Based on these signals, the AI selected the next exercise to maximize learning progress â€” just like an expert therapist would, but at scale.

EMG-based prosthetic systems read electrical signals from residual muscles, but there's a critical problem: beginners produce noisy, inconsistent signals. Muscle patterns can also change over time, making it difficult to retrain the classifier if the user doesn't understand why recognition is poor

Traditional rehabilitation takes an unfocused approach â€” gestures are practiced in random or fixed order, with no consideration for which movements are actually difficult for the user or confusing for the algorithm. Rethinking How We Teach Prosthetic Control

In this project, I tackled a fundamental question: Can we create an intelligent curriculum that adapts to both the user's motor learning needs and the classifier's training requirements?

The Innovation: A Two-Way Learning System

Instead of forcing users through predetermined exercises, we designed an adaptive motor-learning framework that monitors both human performance and machine learning model accuracy in real-time.

The key insight: the order in which gestures are practiced matters enormously. Just as you wouldn't teach a child to read by starting with Shakespeare, prosthetic users shouldn't practice the most confusing gestures first.

Three Teaching Strategies Compared

Random Curriculum (RC): The standard approach used today â€” gestures presented randomly, with no strategic planning. This is our baseline.

User Choice Curriculum (UCC): Users decide which gesture to practice next. This promotes autonomy and engagement but provides no expert guidance on optimal learning sequences.

Gesture Separability Curriculum (GSC): Our adaptive system analyzes the classifier's internal representations and intelligently selects the next gesture that will maximize class separability â€” essentially practicing the movements that are currently most confused, where improvement will have the biggest impact.

Think of GSC as an expert rehabilitation therapist who constantly assesses: "Which movement is this patient struggling with most? Which gesture is the prosthesis misinterpreting? Let's focus there."
